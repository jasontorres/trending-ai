The best team I ever built fired me. Not literally—but they reached a point where my daily involvement became optional. They shipped features, resolved conflicts, made architectural decisions, and onboarded new engineers without me. It was the proudest moment of my career as a leader. Most engineering leaders optimize for being indispensable. The truly effective ones optimize for becoming unnecessary.

This article explores how to build teams with strong autonomy: creating decision-making frameworks that work when you're not in the room, hiring for judgment over credentials, designing feedback loops that self-correct, and knowing when your presence helps vs. when it creates dependency. It's about building systems—cultural and technical—that scale beyond any individual, including yourself.
