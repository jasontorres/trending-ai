Managing distributed teams across Manila and London means 8-hour time zone gaps, different cultural expectations around communication, and the constant challenge of building trust when face-time is rare. I've spent years splitting my time between both cities, running teams that span continents. The key isn't just about Slack and Zoom—it's about designing processes that work asynchronously and building cultures that don't require constant synchronous collaboration to function.

This article covers the practical and cultural aspects of distributed engineering leadership: designing async workflows that don't bottleneck on time zones, running effective remote standups and code reviews, building team cohesion when half the team has never met in person, balancing fairness in compensation across different cost-of-living markets, and the communication patterns that build trust remotely. We'll also explore the unexpected benefits of distributed teams and why some of my best engineering cultures emerged from teams that rarely saw each other.
