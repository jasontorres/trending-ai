Silicon Valley thinks it's building the future, but it's really just building solutions for SF, NYC, and London—and assuming they'll work everywhere else. Meanwhile, Southeast Asia is solving problems that don't exist in the West: mobile-first everything (because desktop never happened), payment systems designed for the unbanked, and logistics networks for dense urban environments with chaotic traffic. The innovation isn't a worse copy of Western tech—it's a different paradigm entirely.

This essay explores the unique characteristics of the Southeast Asian tech ecosystem: why super-apps dominate, how different infrastructure constraints shape product decisions, the role of government and regulation in shaping tech adoption, and the opportunities Western founders miss by assuming their solutions are universal. We'll also examine successful SEA companies that tried to expand West and failed (and why), and Western companies that failed to adapt to SEA markets.
