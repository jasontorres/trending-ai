Game jams are startups compressed into 48 hours: you have an idea, a deadline, and the certainty that whatever you planned will be too ambitious. You'll cut features, hack together terrible code, and ship something that barely works. And somehow, that constraint produces better results than months of careful planning. I've done dozens of game jams, and each one taught me something about building software under pressure that applies far beyond games.

This article breaks down the lessons from hackathons and game jams: how artificial constraints force creative solutions, why scope management is more important than technical skill, the power of timeboxing decisions, and how to identify the core mechanic (or feature) that makes or breaks the experience. We'll also explore the parallels between a 48-hour game jam and launching a startup MVP, and why the ability to ship something imperfect quickly is often more valuable than the ability to architect something perfect slowly.
