My calendar is chaos: morning standups about fintech microservices, lunch spent debugging a Unity physics system, afternoon architecture reviews, evening building a pixel-art roguelike in Godot. People ask how I context-switch between enterprise development and indie game dev. The secret? They're not different. Both are about managing complexity, creating tight feedback loops, and building systems that respond predictably to inputs. The real difference is that games let you see immediately when you've failed.

This article explores how context-switching between drastically different types of development makes you better at both: what enterprise software teaches you about architecture and state management that applies to game systems, what game development teaches you about user feedback and iterative design that applies to enterprise products, and why the discipline of shipping a game solo makes you a better teammate on large engineering orgs. It's also about the creative renewal that comes from building in completely different paradigms.
