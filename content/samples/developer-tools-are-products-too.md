Engineers treat "product thinking" like it's something that applies to everyone else—designers care about UX, PMs care about features, but we just build tools that work. Except they don't work, not really. We build CLI tools with 47 flags, APIs with inconsistent naming, and libraries with documentation that assumes you already know how to use them. Developer tools need product thinking just as much as consumer apps. Maybe more.

This article explores how to apply product discipline to developer tools: understanding your user's workflow (not just their technical requirements), designing APIs that are hard to misuse, writing documentation that reduces time-to-first-success, and measuring actual usage instead of GitHub stars. We'll look at examples from tools that got this right (Stripe, Vercel) and tools that didn't (most internal platforms). Building for developers doesn't mean ignoring UX—it means understanding that engineers' time is your product's most expensive resource.
