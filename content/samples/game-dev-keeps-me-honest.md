After a decade of building enterprise platforms, fintech systems, and civic tech infrastructure, I realized I'd forgotten why I fell in love with programming. Everything was architected, reviewed, deployed through CI/CD pipelines, and monitored with observability tools. It was professional and impressive and utterly joyless. Then I started building games again—small, stupid, personal projects in Unity and Godot—and remembered what it felt like to code for the pure joy of creation.

This essay is about why game development matters for professional engineers: how building something playful resets your relationship with complexity, why shipping a janky game is more fulfilling than perfectly architecting a microservice, and what game dev teaches you about immediate feedback loops and player experience that applies to building any software. It's also about giving yourself permission to build things that don't scale, don't have product-market fit, and don't need to exist for any reason except that you wanted to make them.
